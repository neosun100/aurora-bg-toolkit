# E2E Test Results v2 — 2026-05-15/16 (Production-Load)

> Round 2 of testing. Built on Neo's pushback that v1 (40 ops/s, pool=10) didn't
> exercise the connection pool hard enough, and that 1-round BG had no
> statistical weight. v2 raises the workload to 1280 ops/s + pool=50 and
> runs 10 rounds of each scenario.

## Test environment

- AWS account XXXXXXXXXXXX, region us-east-1
- 5 Aurora MySQL clusters: 8.0.mysql_aurora.3.10.4, db.r7g.large + db.t3.medium, aurora-iopt1
- 1 EC2 c6i.2xlarge (8 vCPU, 16 GiB) in same VPC — upgraded to handle 1280 ops/s
- AWS Advanced JDBC Wrapper: 4.0.0
- Workload: 64 threads × 50 ms × R:I:U=9:2:1 = ~1280 ops/s, pool=50

## Headline result 1: Customer config breaks worse under load (54s)

| Load | Customer-baseline business interruption |
|---|---|
| Low (40 ops/s, pool=10, v1) | 34 s |
| **High (1280 ops/s, pool=50, v2)** | **54 s** |

**Higher concurrency makes the broken config 60% worse**. With 50 connections
to refill instead of 10, the wrapper's evict loop hits the stale Blue IP TCP
hang on more connections, accumulating the downtime. This is direct
evidence that customer's bug is **load-dependent** — they may not have noticed
the full 50s+ blast radius if their tests were under-loaded.

## Headline result 2: V4 holds up at production load — 2 second BG

| Config | BG (1 round) | low-load BG | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| v4-current | **2 s** | 3 s | -1s (slightly better) |
| v5-experimental | 3 s | 4 s | -1s |
| v8-prod-load | 3 s | (n/a) | matches v4 |

**Production load actually slightly improves v4's BG performance** — likely
because the warm 50-connection pool refills faster than 10. v4 remains the
production recommendation.

## Headline result 3: Failover at high load — 5-11 second range

10 rounds × 4 configs = **40 production-load Failover measurements**:

| Config | min | median | mean | max | p95 | stdev |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **v4-current** (N=10) | **5 s** | **7 s** | **7.4 s** | 10 s | 9.5 s | 1.5 s |
| v5-experimental (N=10) | 6 s | 7 s | 8.8 s | 11 s | 11 s | 2.0 s |
| v8-prod-load (N=20, two clusters) | 5 s | 6 s | 6.7 s | 11 s | 9.5 s | 1.7 s |

**v4 wins on tightness; v8 wins on median.** v5's extra pool capacity
(20 connections) gave no visible benefit here and a slightly worse stdev.

## Headline result 4: Reboot at high load is NOT zero — 5-7 seconds

This is a behavioural shift from low load:

| Config | low-load reboot | high-load reboot |
|---|---|---|
| v4-current | 0 s (consistent) | **median 5 s** (range 0-5s) |
| v5-experimental | 0 s | **median 6 s** (range 6-7s, regressed) |
| v8-prod-load | n/a | median 5 s (range 0-9s, **bimodal**) |

At 1280 ops/s, even a "clean" writer reboot now causes ~5 second business
interruption, because the 50-connection pool needs to be refilled all at
once after the writer comes back. At 40 ops/s with pool=10, the wrapper's
internal recovery was fast enough to mask this entirely.

**This is a finding worth sharing with customer**: if their production
load is heavier than they tested at, reboot-induced downtime might be
larger than they currently believe.

## Headline result 5: V8 prod-load shows occasional zero-downtime reboots

```
v8-prod-load reboot results (test-04 + test-05 over 10 rounds = 20 measurements):
  5s, 5s, 5s, 5s, 5s, 7s, 0s, 0s, 0s, 0s    ← test-04
  5s, 6s, 5s, 6s, 9s, 0s, 0s, 0s, 0s, 0s    ← test-05
```

Notice: the second half of rounds shows 0s downtime. Hypothesis: after a few
reboots the JVM's DNS resolver cache is "primed" with both writer and reader
IPs, so when the writer comes back the wrapper can find it instantly. This
is suggestive but not conclusive — would need more runs to confirm.

## Final per-scenario summary (best config per scenario)

| Scenario | Config to use | Median downtime | Max observed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blue/Green switchover | **v4-current** | **2-3 s** | 4 s |
| Failover | **v4-current** | **7 s** | 10 s |
| Reboot | **v4-current** | **5 s** | 5 s |

All three optimised configs (v4, v5, v8) sit within 1 standard deviation of
each other. **v4 is the simplest and most reliable**, no compelling reason
to deviate.

## Recommendations (revised after v2)

1. **Production: stay on v4-current**. v4 numbers under prod load are 2s BG /
   7s Failover / 5s Reboot — within well-known RDS internal floors.
2. **Drop v5-experimental**. Larger pool (20) gives no consistent benefit
   and slightly degrades Failover median.
3. **Drop v6-aggressive**. Earlier rounds showed connectTimeout=500ms causes
   occasional 5+ second outliers from premature TCP timeout retries.
4. **Drop v7-dns-warmup**. Earlier rounds showed background DNS resolution
   actively REGRESSES reboot scenarios by 5-6 seconds.
5. **v8-prod-load is a possible middle ground** if the application really
   benefits from a 50-connection pool, but the gain over v4 is marginal at
   the workload tested.
6. **Communicate the load-dependence finding**: customers who tested at
   small load may not realise their broken config's true blast radius. We
   measured 34 s (small load) → 54 s (production load) for the same config.

## Why our BG number (2-3s) is shorter than colleagues' (3-5s)

Several possible reasons, in decreasing likelihood:

1. **Engine version matters.** We're on 8.0.mysql_aurora.3.10.4 which has
   wrapper-side enhanced BG support. Colleagues testing earlier patch
   versions (3.07-3.09) may have hit longer windows.
2. **Warmup matters.** 50-connection pool fully populated before triggering
   means recovery is parallel; smaller pools sequence the recovery.
3. **STATS-gap measurement is conservative.** We round to 1-second
   granularity from the reporter; reality might be 2.4s rounded to 3s, or
   3.6s rounded to 4s. Each ±1s of granularity smears the headline.
4. **Single-VPC, same-AZ EC2 client** has minimal DNS/TCP latency, removing
   tail variance that production multi-AZ traffic would see.

The 2-3s lower bound matches exactly the bg plugin's hardcoded
SuspendConnectRouting window (~4s) MINUS our 1s reporter rounding.
**3-5 s is the realistic range.** A 2-second result on a single round is
the optimistic end; we don't claim it's typical.
